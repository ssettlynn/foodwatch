What We Predict

One dataset, three questions

From the same socioeconomic signals, FoodWatch answers three different questions about next year — each a different kind of machine-learning task.

Notice the pattern: predicting the level (tier or value) is relatively easy because hunger changes slowly year-to-year. Predicting the direction of change is genuinely hard — and that is exactly the early-warning question that matters most.
One Year vs Five Years

The further ahead we look, the harder it gets

We built two models: one predicts next year, one predicts five years ahead — both from the same ten signals. Accuracy drops with distance, and that drop is itself a finding.

Accuracy by horizon (test set)

Macro-F1 falls from about 0.96 (1 year) to 0.83 (5 years). Why? Over five years, four times as many countries change tier, and there are fewer complete records to learn from.

How often does the risk tier change?

Tiers are sticky year-to-year (about 4% change), which is why "same as last year" is a strong baseline. Over five years, roughly 16% change — more room for a model to add value.
The Scoreboard

Four models, tested honestly

Every model is scored on data it never saw during training. "High-recall" is the share of truly high-risk countries the model catches — the number that matters most for early warning.

Why The Model Decides

What actually drives the risk

A prediction nobody understands is a prediction nobody trusts. These bars show how much each signal moves the forecast — and how that shifts as we look further ahead.

Current hunger dominates the one-year view. Over five years its weight fades and population growth rises — near-term risk is about "who is struggling now", long-term risk is more about structural pressure.
Patterns In The Data

Conditions that travel with hunger

Beyond the models, we mined the data for combinations of conditions that occur together. "Lift" tells you how many times more likely serious hunger is when a pattern holds.

The last rule explains something the charts could not: import dependence looks harmless on its own because rich import-dependent countries are food-secure. It only raises risk when combined with low income — a pattern single-variable analysis cannot see.
