The Crisis

A decade of progress, reversed in two years

From 2010 to 2018, global undernourishment fell from 10.3% to 9.3% — steady, hard-won progress. Then COVID-19 collapsed economies, and the 2022 food-price shock hit import bills. By 2022 the global average was back near 9.9%.

Our data shows something crucial: economies crashed in 2020, but hunger peaked in 2022. That two-year lag is exactly the window early-warning systems exist for.

How It Works

Three tiers. One year ahead.

Every country-year is scored on the UN FAO's undernourishment bands. Our models read this year's signals to predict next year's tier.

Low

Undernourishment below 5%. Food access is broadly secure; monitoring continues.

<5%

Medium

5–15%. Vulnerable to shocks — the tier where early action is cheapest and most effective.

5–15

High

Above 15%. Concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa and crisis-affected states. Urgent attention.

Explainable AI

Not just what — why

A warning nobody trusts is a warning nobody acts on. Every FoodWatch prediction ships with a SHAP explanation showing exactly which factors pushed a country toward risk — hunger level, food supply, income, inflation.

Example: Afghanistan is flagged High because undernourishment sits at 27.5% and food supply adequacy is weak — the model shows its reasoning the way an analyst would.

The Story In Data

Fourteen years, four turning points

2010

Global mean undernourishment at 10.3%. The long decline begins — driven by growth across Asia.

2018

The best year in our panel: 9.29%. South Asia nearly halves its hunger rate since 2010.

2020

COVID-19: global growth collapses to −4.6%. High-risk countries jump from 33 to 39.

2022

Food-price shock. Hunger peaks at 9.86% — two years after the crash. The lag proves predictable.

2024

Our forecast: 34 countries at High risk. Somalia, DR Congo and Madagascar flagged with near-certain confidence.

Questions

Honest answers

How accurate are the forecasts?

On unseen years (2021–2023, including COVID aftermath and the price shock) our best models reach 96% accuracy and 0.96 macro-F1. Crucially, no high-risk country was ever misclassified as low-risk — the worst kind of error for an early-warning system never occurred in testing.

Risk tiers rarely change — so what does the model add?

True: about 96% of countries keep their tier from one year to the next, so "same as last year" is a strong guess. The model's real value is the explanation — SHAP shows which pressures are building in each country, which a naive guess can never do.

Is the data up to date?

FAO releases finalized data with a 1–2 year lag, so the panel ends in 2023 and forecasts 2024. The pipeline is fully retrainable: when new data drops, one script refresh updates everything.

Who is this for?

Students, analysts, journalists and anyone who wants to understand where food insecurity is heading — built as an academic data-mining project on 100% open data, with methods documented end to end.
